The Alan Turing Institute
Year 2017 in numbers

The Alan Turing Institute is the United Kingdom national institute for data sciences, founded in 2015. It is named after Alan Turing, the British mathematician and computing pioneer. Since getting started in 2015, the institute hired its first Research Fellows, published numerous recorded talks, over 50 publications and started new research projects that aim to advance the fields of data science, machine learning and many more.

Here, we present information about the Alan Turing Institute in a fun interactive way. We are using tools developed as part of The Gamma project, which make it possible to present data in a transparent way. Behind every chart on this page, there is source code that links the chart to the primary data source and lets you explore the data further. The visualizations are inspired by the You Draw It series by New York Times and let you make your own guess about the data before showing the actual numbers. How many of the facts about the Alan Turing Institute can you guess correctly?

Fellows from partner universities

Talks at the Alan Turing Institute

The Alan Turing Institute has been hosting a number of talk series and posting the recording on YouTube throughout the year. Videos from the Fellow Short Talks series feature the current research of the Research Fellows and Falculty Fellows. The Turing Lectures series contains invited talks from researchers across the world. The chart below shows the aggregate number of video views for all the talks posted on YouTube.

Note that the chart below is calculated from a data dump from YouTube and so you can click "view source code" and explore the data on your own. For example, try looking at the number of subscribers rather than the number of views!

When are videos published

Let's now have a more detailed look at the YouTube video views. The bubble chart shows when each video was published together with the total number of views since it was published. This means that older videos had more time to get more views.

You can see huge spike in activity around October 2016 when regular talk series started and then a break around Christmas 2016. Since then, activity has been more regular.

Now the game part! The line chart below shows total video views per day. This jumps up and down, so the line chart shows average values over two weeks. Can you guess the rest of the line?

As with the previous charts, you can click "open source code" to see how the visualization is created. Change the window size on the second line from 15 to 1 or 30 to see how it changes!
